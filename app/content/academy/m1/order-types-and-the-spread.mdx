This lesson is the closest thing to free money the app can offer: not a strategy, just the plumbing of how an order fills. Understanding it costs nothing and saves real money, which is why Part 6 of the research calls execution knowledge an edge relative to ignorance rather than an edge over the market.

## Two orders, two trade-offs

Every trade is placed as one of two basic order types.

- A **market order** says "fill me now, at whatever price is available". It trades certainty of *execution* for uncertainty of *price*. It will almost always fill immediately — but at a price the reader does not get to choose.
- A **limit order** says "fill me only at this price or better". It trades certainty of *price* for uncertainty of *execution*. It protects the reader from a bad fill, at the cost that it may not fill at all if the market moves away.

Neither is right for every moment. But the app has a default, and it is the limit order — for reasons that come down to the spread.

## The spread is a real cost

At any instant a stock has two quoted prices: the highest price a buyer will pay (the bid) and the lowest price a seller will accept (the ask). The **bid-ask spread** is the gap between them. A market buy pays the ask; a market sell receives the bid. So crossing the spread is a cost paid on every market order, every time — an invisible fee that never appears on a statement.

The gap between the price a reader expected and the price actually filled is **slippage**. Wide spreads and thin liquidity make slippage worse, and a market order absorbs all of it.

## Why the open is the worst time for a market order

Spreads are not constant through the day. They are **widest in the first 30 minutes** and tighten as the session settles:

- over **20 basis points** on large S&P 500 names at the open, versus
- under **5 basis points** late in the day.

That is roughly a fourfold difference in cost for the identical trade, decided only by *when* it is sent. A market order at 9:31am pays the spread at its very widest. The same order mid-afternoon pays a quarter as much. This is the opening-30-minutes volatility from the previous lesson, felt as a bill.

## The default: prefer limit orders

So the plain rule the app teaches is this:

- **Prefer a limit order by default.** Set the worst price acceptable and let the order wait. This caps the spread cost instead of paying it blind.
- **Avoid market orders at the open**, where the spread — and therefore the slippage — is at its widest.
- If a market order is genuinely needed, the deep, tight **closing auction** is a far cheaper moment than the open.

None of this predicts direction. It only stops a reader from handing back, in execution cost, whatever small edge a good decision might have earned.

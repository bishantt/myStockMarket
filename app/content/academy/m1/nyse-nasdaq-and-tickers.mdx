Before any of the honesty machinery makes sense, it helps to know the plainest facts about where US stocks live. This lesson covers the two main exchanges and the short code that names each stock.

## The two main exchanges

Almost every US stock a reader will meet trades on one of two exchanges.

- The **New York Stock Exchange (NYSE)** is the older one. Its heritage is the auction floor: buyers and sellers meeting through a designated market maker, historically a human specialist, who helped set a fair opening and closing price. Much of that is electronic now, but the auction model still shapes how the NYSE opens and closes.
- **Nasdaq** grew up electronic. It began as a dealer network — competing market makers posting the prices at which they would buy and sell — rather than a single auction. It never had a physical trading floor.

The distinction is real history, not marketing. But for a reader who holds positions for months or years, it changes almost nothing about the outcome.

## What a ticker is

A **ticker** is the short code that names a listed security — usually one to five letters. It is the unambiguous handle that an exchange, a broker, and this app all use to point at the same stock. A company name can be typed ten ways; a ticker cannot. When the app shows a base rate or a scan, the ticker is how it knows exactly which security the history belongs to.

By loose convention NYSE tickers ran one to three letters and Nasdaq tickers four, but that rule has frayed and is not worth relying on. Treat the ticker as an identifier, nothing more.

## Where ETFs list

Exchange-traded funds are listed securities too, and many of them list on **NYSE Arca (ARCA)** — an all-electronic exchange within the NYSE group built for funds. So a reader will often see a fund's ticker attached to ARCA rather than the main NYSE or Nasdaq. It is the same idea: a venue where the security trades and a ticker that names it.

## Why the app rarely mentions the venue

The app cares about the company and its price history, not the building the shares trade in. Listing venue is not a catalyst, not a pattern, and not a base rate. So it is worth knowing the words — NYSE, Nasdaq, ARCA, ticker — and then setting the venue question aside. For a long-term reader, where a stock lists is trivia, and the app treats it that way.

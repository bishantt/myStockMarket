Four predictable mistakes show up in the trading records of ordinary investors. The research measured each one and put a number on its cost. None of them is exotic, and none waits: all four arrive in the first month a person starts trading. The app cannot stop a user from making them, but it can refuse to encourage them, and it can hold up a mirror so the user sees the pattern in their own account.

## Tax one: the disposition effect

The **disposition effect** is selling winners and keeping losers. It feels like discipline — "lock in the gain, give the loser time to recover" — and it is exactly backwards. In the household records, the winners that were sold went on to beat the losers that were kept by about **3.4 percentage points** over the following year. The tax is the gap between the stock let go and the stock held.

The app fights this with friction, not advice. A cooling-off prompt sits between the impulse and the sale, and stops are set in advance so the exit rule is written before the emotion arrives. Neither tells the user what to do; both slow the reflex down long enough for the plan to win.

## Tax two: lottery preference

**Lottery preference** is the pull toward cheap stocks with a tiny chance of a huge payoff — the "jackpot" names. The skew is what attracts: a small stake that might multiply. The measured cost of tilting a portfolio this way is about **2 to 3 percentage points a year**, paid steadily for the rare thrill that mostly does not come.

The app does not surface these names as opportunities. Instead it flags **lottery risk** — it marks the high-skew profile as a warning, not a feature, so the thing that attracts the eye is labelled as the thing that drains the account.

## Tax three: overconfidence

**Overconfidence** shows up as trading too much. The more certain a person feels, the more they trade, and the more they trade, the more cost they pay. The clearest measurement is a gender gap: across US households, **men traded 45 percent more than women** and paid about **0.9 percentage points more per year** in drag for the extra activity. The belief that one can time entries and exits is expensive, and the expense is roughly proportional to the confidence.

The app answers with a **trade-frequency mirror**: it simply shows the user how often they trade. No lecture, no score — just the count, held up plainly, because most people who overtrade do not know they do it.

## Tax four: attention herding

**Attention herding** is buying whatever is being bought — the trending name, the crowd's pick. It is the most directly punished of the four. The most crowd-bought names earned about **negative 4.7 percent abnormal returns over the following 20 days**. Chasing attention does not merely fail to help; it points almost exactly at the wrong trade.

This is why the app has **no trending surface, no "most bought" list, no popularity ranking** of any kind. A feature that showed the crowd's favourites would be a machine for delivering this exact tax to its users. The honest design choice is to not build it, and the app does not.

## What the four have in common

Each tax has a measured cost, and each has a surface — or a deliberate absence of one — that answers it:

- disposition effect, about 3.4pp/year — the cooling-off prompt and pre-set stops
- lottery preference, about 2 to 3pp/year — the lottery-risk flag instead of a jackpot list
- overconfidence, about 0.9pp/year — the trade-frequency mirror
- attention herding, about −4.7% over 20 days — no trending or most-bought surface at all

The app does not claim to cure any of them. It claims something smaller and truer: it will not profit from them, and it will show the user the evidence in their own behaviour. The [track record](/track-record) holds the app to the same standard it asks of the user.

Buying a stock and later selling it is a **round trip**. It is the smallest complete unit of trading, and it is the honest place to measure cost. The price on the screen is not one number — it is two.

## The spread is a price you pay twice

At any moment a stock has two quoted prices: the **bid** (what a buyer will pay) and the **ask** (what a seller will accept). The gap between them is the **bid-ask spread**. A market order to buy fills at the ask; a market order to sell fills at the bid. So the round trip crosses the spread once going in and once coming out.

> Buy at the ask, sell at the bid. The spread is paid on both legs — twice per round trip.

This is true even when the broker advertises zero commission. Commission-free does not mean cost-free. The spread is the cost, and it is collected quietly inside the fill price rather than billed on a statement.

## Effective spread is the real number

The quoted spread is what the screen shows. The **effective spread** is what actually happened: the difference between the price paid and the mid-point of the quote at the moment of the order. It captures the part of the cost that the quote alone does not — including any **slippage**, the small gap between the price expected and the price filled. The effective spread is the honest measure of what a round trip cost, because it is measured from real fills, not from the tidy number on the screen.

## The open is the expensive window

Spreads are not constant through the day. They are widest in the first 30 minutes after the open, when the overnight order flow is still being sorted out:

- On large S&P 500 names, the effective spread can run **over 20bp** at the open.
- Later in the day the same names settle to **under 5bp**.

A basis point (bp) is one hundredth of a percent, so 20bp is 0.20%. That difference is why a market order fired at the opening bell pays the most it will pay all day. A patient limit order, or simply waiting past the first half hour, avoids the widest part of the spread.

## Small costs compound

The reason any of this matters is repetition. A spread that costs a fraction of a percent looks trivial on a single round trip. But an active trader does not make one round trip — they make many, and the cost is paid on every one. What is invisible per trade becomes the dominant drag over a year of trading.

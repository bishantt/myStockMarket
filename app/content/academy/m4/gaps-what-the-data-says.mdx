This is the lesson the app keeps as its worked example. If you learn to check one claim against a base rate here, you can do it to any claim the market throws at you. The claim we will check is the most repeated piece of chart folklore there is: **"gaps always fill."**

## What a gap is

A **gap** is a jump between one session's close and the next session's open with no trading in between. A stock closes at one price, news or overnight orders arrive, and it opens 3% higher the next morning. The empty space on the chart is the gap. "Filling" the gap means price later trades back through that empty space to the prior close.

The folklore says this fill is close to inevitable — that a gap is a rubber band that snaps back. It sounds tidy. Now check it.

## Step one: name the claim precisely

"Gaps always fill" hides two vague words. **Always** — over what horizon? By the end of the day? The month? Ever? And **fill** measured how? A precise claim can be tested; a vague one just feels true. The moment you demand a horizon, the "always" starts to wobble.

## Step two: find the real evidence

The app's research ledger grades this claim **folklore**, and here is why.

- The main **peer-reviewed** gap study finds **momentum in the gap's direction, not reversal**. On average, a stock that gaps up tends to keep drifting up, and one that gaps down tends to keep falling. That is the opposite of "fills back to where it started."
- The famous **"70 to 90% of gaps fill"** figure has **no traceable source**. It circulates from blog to blog with no study behind it. A number nobody can source is not evidence.

## Step three: expose the base-rate trick

Even the fills that do happen are mostly a **base-rate artifact**. Given enough time, price wanders back through almost any level at least once — that is just what prices do. So "most gaps eventually fill" can be true and still tell you nothing, because *most levels* eventually get revisited whether there was a gap or not. Without a fixed horizon and a comparison against how often ordinary levels get revisited, a high fill rate is meaningless. This is exactly what the **always-up baseline** exists to catch: a number only means something measured against what would have happened anyway.

## Step four: state the honest reading

Put the three findings together:

1. The peer-reviewed evidence points to **continuation**, not reversal — the folklore is aimed the wrong way.
2. The headline "70 to 90%" statistic has **no source**.
3. Eventual fills are a **base-rate artifact** with no horizon and no baseline.

The honest verdict is that "gaps always fill" is **folklore** — not an insult, just information. The word tells you the wider evidence does not hold the claim up.

## What the app does instead

The app does not trade the gap on the folklore. It grades the gap scan **folklore**, and it treats a gap as a **catalyst context signal**: a gap usually means real news arrived overnight, so the useful move is to go find the news, not to bet on a snap-back. When the app flags a gap with a catalyst, this lesson is the doorway it opens. Read the gap, check the base rate over a fixed horizon, hold it against the always-up baseline — and only then decide the claim means anything.

Volume is the count of shares that changed hands. On its own a raw number — a few million shares — means nothing, because a big stock always trades more than a small one. To read volume honestly you have to compare it with itself.

## What RVOL is

**RVOL**, or relative volume, does exactly that. It measures today's volume against the stock's own recent average for the same point in the trading day. It is a ratio, not a raw count:

- **RVOL near 1** — an ordinary day; activity is about normal for this name.
- **RVOL of 2 or 3** — unusual; two to three times the shares are trading than is typical.

Framed this way, RVOL answers one clean question: is more attention than usual arriving on this stock right now? That question is worth asking. What you do with the answer is where honesty is needed.

## What unusual volume is worth

The temptation is to read a high RVOL as a buy signal. The research does not support that. The app's ledger grades the **high-volume return premium mixed**, and the detail matters:

- Unusual volume genuinely **carries information**. In the strongest deciles, high-volume stocks earned about **0.5% per month** from 1963 to 1996. That is a real, measured tilt.
- But the effect is **concentrated in small caps with the widest bid-ask spreads**. Those are precisely the stocks where it costs the most to trade. The spread quietly consumes the edge.

So the honest summary is: **information, not a strategy**. High RVOL tells you that something has drawn a crowd to this name. It does not, by itself, tell you which way the price will go, and it does not survive the cost of acting on it in the stocks where it is strongest.

## How the app uses it

This is why the app treats unusual volume as **context**, never a recommendation. A spike in RVOL is a reason to go look for a cause — an earnings report, a news catalyst, an analyst move — not a reason to trade. And if there is no cause to find, the app says so plainly rather than inventing one: most large moves in a single name have no clean explanation.

When the app surfaces a name on unusual volume, this lesson is the doorway. Read the RVOL as a flag that says "attention arrived here" — then check what the attention is about, and hold it against the base rate and the always-up baseline before it means anything at all.

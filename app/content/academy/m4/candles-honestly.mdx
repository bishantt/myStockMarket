A candlestick is two different things wearing one costume. One of them is honest data display. The other is a set of named patterns that the research does not support. Keeping them apart is the whole lesson.

## What a candle encodes

A single candlestick draws four numbers for one period of trading:

- the **open** — the first traded price of the period
- the **high** — the highest price reached
- the **low** — the lowest price reached
- the **close** — the last traded price

The body runs between the open and the close; the thin wicks reach out to the high and the low. That is all a candle claims. It is a compact, honest picture of where price started, how far it stretched each way, and where it ended. Read that way, a candle is just a chart, and the app is glad to show it.

## Where the trouble starts

The trouble begins when a candle stops being a picture and becomes a **pattern** with a fortune attached — a "hammer", a "doji", an "engulfing" shape said to signal what comes next. That is a directional claim, and directional claims have to answer to the evidence.

## What the research found

The app's research ledger grades candlestick patterns **weak**, and it labels the grade with an honest word: **contested-negative**.

Here is what that word is carrying:

- The main test looked at 28 candlestick patterns on Dow stocks from 1992 to 2002. It found no value **before** trading costs — and costs only make a marginal edge worse, never better.
- An independent replication on the Japanese market, the home of candlestick charting, also came back **negative**.
- But the result is not unanimous. A few three-day patterns do show small profits under alternative exit rules. Those profits are specification-sensitive — they depend on exactly which exit you pick — and are unlikely to survive real retail costs.

## Why "contested-negative" is a feature, not a dodge

It would be easier to stamp candlestick patterns "debunked" and move on. The app does not, because that would be its own kind of dishonesty. The truthful summary is: the weight of the evidence is negative, one corner of it is not, and the exceptions are fragile. "Contested-negative" says exactly that.

This is the same honesty machinery you see everywhere else in the app. A base rate carries a confidence interval instead of a single confident number; a pattern carries its evidence grade instead of a verdict. A grade that admits its own soft spots is doing its job. So read a candle for its four numbers, and treat the pattern names as folklore-adjacent decoration — interesting to know, not something to trade on.

Some technical ideas are folklore. This one is not — or not entirely. Support and resistance at round numbers is the best-understood mechanism in all of technical analysis, which makes it a good place to see what "mixed" really means.

## The idea, stated plainly

**Support** is a price level where falling prices have tended to stop and turn up. **Resistance** is a level where rising prices have tended to stall. The claim is that certain levels act like floors and ceilings.

For most levels this is storytelling drawn after the fact. But at **round numbers** — a whole dollar, a big round index level — there is a real reason it can happen.

## The mechanism the data can see

People place orders at round numbers. Limit orders, stop orders, and option strikes all cluster there because round numbers are where humans naturally aim. That clustering is real, and it leaves a mark: prices bounce at round-number levels about **4.6 percentage points more often** than at arbitrary levels nearby.

That is why the app's research ledger grades support and resistance **mixed** rather than folklore. There is a genuine, measured effect with a mechanism you can name.

## Why mixed, and not supported

Read the rest of the finding before reaching for it:

- The horizon is **intraday**. The extra bounces show up inside the day, not over the days and weeks a base rate on this app usually measures.
- **No cost-surviving strategy is documented.** A 4.6-point edge in bounce frequency is not the same as money after spreads and commissions. Nobody has shown a rule that turns this into net profit.

So the honest one-line summary is: it **explains levels; it is not tradable**. The clustering tells you why a chart pauses where it does. It does not hand you a trade.

## The 52-week high, and a folklore correction

A special round-ish level is the **52-week high** — the highest price a stock reached in the past year. Proximity to it is its own signal, and the ledger grades it **mixed**: about **0.45% per month** in-sample from 1963 to 2001, but it **fails in Japan** and weakened after it was published.

Now the correction, because the desk lore here is exactly backwards. Traders often say "new highs are bearish — what goes up must come down". The evidence says the opposite: new highs historically predicted **continuation**, not reversal. A stock near its 52-week high tended, on average, to keep drifting up, not to roll over. So "new highs are bearish" is **backwards folklore**, and the app labels it as such.

Even corrected, this is a **context signal only** — something that colours the reading, never a reason to buy on its own. That fits the tier language: at best "note it; check the weakeners".

When the app flags a name near its 52-week high, this lesson is the doorway it points to. Read the level for what it is — a place where orders and attention cluster — not as a promise about tomorrow. The related pattern lives over in [/scans](/scans).

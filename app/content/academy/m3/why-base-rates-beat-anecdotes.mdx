A winning trade is easy to remember and easy to trust. It is also one data point. This lesson is about why one vivid outcome should never outweigh the whole record, and why the app is built to keep the record in front of you.

## One trade is an anecdote; the class is the base rate

An **anecdote** is a single case that stuck in memory — the trade that worked, told as if it proves the method. The problem is not that it is false. The trade really did win. The problem is that one outcome cannot tell you whether the method works, because it says nothing about the times the same method lost. Those losses are quieter and easier to forget, so memory hands you a flattering sample.

A **base rate** fixes this by counting the whole **reference class**: every case the setup produced, wins and losses together, over a stated group and horizon. It is the anecdote's honest replacement. Where the story shows you the one that worked, the base rate shows you how often it worked out of how many tries. That is the only fair way to judge a method, and it is the number the app puts on every card.

## The census is the base rate for active trading

For active retail trading, the reference class has been counted directly, not sampled. Two national census studies followed every trader:

- **Taiwan**, tracking every day trader over 15 years: fewer than **1%** were persistently profitable net of fees, and the few winners showed skill they already had, not skill they learned.
- **Brazil**, tracking those who day-traded **300 or more sessions**: **97%** lost money, and there was no evidence that experience improved outcomes — directly contradicting the promise that active trading is a craft you get better at.

That is the base rate. It is not a warning about doing it badly; it is the whole class's record of doing it at all. Any single trader's winning week is an anecdote drawn from that class, and the class loses.

## Risk math first, then patterns

This is the lesson that closes the module, so it says the point plainly. Learn the risk math first — sizing, stops, expectancy, and drawdown, the four lessons before this one — and only then look at patterns. A pattern is worth studying once you can size it with a [half-Kelly cap](/academy/m3/position-sizing-before-patterns), place a stop by [volatility](/academy/m3/stops-and-invalidation), and read its [expectancy and its worst drawdown](/academy/m3/expectancy-and-drawdown-math) honestly. Reached in that order, a pattern is one more input to weigh against its base rate. Reached first, it is just a better story to lose money with. The patterns are unlocked now — approach them with the risk math already in hand.

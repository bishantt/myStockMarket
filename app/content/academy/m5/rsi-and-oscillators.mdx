Oscillators are indicators that swing back and forth inside a bounded range, meant to flag when a move has gone "too far". Three are unavoidable in a beginner's first month. Here is each one plainly, followed by what the record actually shows.

## The three you will meet

- **RSI**, the relative strength index, compares the size of recent up-moves to recent down-moves and reports the result on a 0-to-100 scale. The convention calls readings above 70 "overbought" and below 30 "oversold". Those are just names painted on a smoothed ratio; the ratio does not know what price will do next.
- **MACD**, moving-average convergence divergence, is the gap between a fast and a slow moving average, plotted with a signal line. When the two lines cross, chart readers call it a momentum shift. It is the same moving-average arithmetic from the previous lesson, dressed in a different chart.
- **Bollinger bands** wrap a moving average in an upper and lower band set a couple of standard deviations away. Price touching a band is read as "stretched". The bands widen when recent volatility rises and narrow when it falls — that part is honest description; the trading rules attached to it are the disputed part.

## What the evidence finds

The app grades RSI and the standard oscillators **weak**, and the finding is blunt:

> At standard settings, RSI and the common oscillators show no significant net profits in developed markets. The positive results that circulate come from parameter tuning — searching after the fact for the window lengths and thresholds that would have worked on a particular history.

That searching is the same trap the golden-cross lesson describes. Try enough settings on one dataset and one combination will look profitable by chance. It is data-snooping, and it does not repeat on fresh data.

The ledger's retail-capture column answers **none**. Even where a tuned setting looks good on paper, it does not survive real trading costs for a private investor. So the honest reading of an oscillator is that it is a way to *describe* a chart — stretched, smoothed, crossed — not a way to beat holding.

Every technique a beginner meets in month one carries a reputation. The reputation is rarely the evidence. The app keeps a ledger of eighteen of these techniques and grades each one against the peer-reviewed record, so the reader can see the gap between what a method is *said* to do and what it has been *shown* to do.

## The four verdicts

Each technique gets one of four **evidence grades**:

- **supported** — the effect holds up out-of-sample in the published literature.
- **mixed** — some real signal, but fragile, context-bound, or already fading.
- **weak** — looked good on the data that discovered it, has not held up elsewhere.
- **folklore** — no real support; a story the desk tells itself.

The word **folklore** is not an insult. It is information. A claim labelled folklore is one the reader will hear repeated with total confidence and which the data does not back.

## The column most guides leave out

Beside the grade sits a second, harsher question: **retail capture?** — can an ordinary individual actually harvest this effect after real costs? These are different questions. A technique can be genuinely real and still impossible for a private trader to keep once spreads and fees are paid. Separating the two is the point of the ledger. "It works in the papers" and "you can keep the money" are not the same claim.

## The honest spread

The eighteen entries do not line up neatly on either side. They spread out, and the spread itself is the lesson.

- At the strong end, **volatility forecasting** is genuinely **supported** — the robust one, still working after seventeen years. **Cross-sectional momentum** is also **supported**, replicated across eight asset classes and two centuries. But momentum's retail-capture answer is essentially no: only low-cost funds can harvest it, and it carries the ugliest tail in the whole ledger — roughly −73% over March–May 2009 and −91% over July–August 1932, gross. Any presentation of momentum without its crash profile is marketing.
- In the wide middle sits most chart lore, **mixed to weak**. Round-number support and resistance is mixed and explainable but not tradable. 52-week-high proximity is mixed and, notably, points the *opposite* way to the folk belief — new highs historically predicted continuation, not a fall. Moving-average and oscillator rules land at weak.
- At the bottom, plainly labelled **folklore**: Fibonacci retracements, Elliott Wave, and "gaps always fill". Bounce rates at Fibonacci zones are statistically indistinguishable from arbitrary zones. Elliott counts are revised after the fact and largely unfalsifiable. And the gap claim reverses the actual finding.

## Where to read it

Every preset scan in the app shows its grade, so the reader never meets a pattern without its verdict attached — open `/scans` to see the grades in place. For a single claim worked all the way through, from folk belief to base rate, read the gap study at `/academy/gaps-what-the-data-says`. It is the ledger's worked example of how to check any claim against the record.
